Automated short answer grading system for teachers


Introduction This course project taught me the importance of designing a product to suit the needs of a target audience. I proposed the idea for this group project because I have both friends and family who are educators. We conducted interviews to assess their needs for the product, and got their feedback on initial prototypes.

My roles on this project were:
  • Evaluating user needs and feedback
  • Handwriting to text functionality, via OCR
This project taught me more about group coding, and adapting 'off the shelf' OCR models to suit our needs. The most valuable things I learned were about tailoring a product to meet specific customer needs, rather than my idea of their needs. Obtaining user feedback and altering the prototype based on this feedback was a pivotal learning opportunity.

More details about the project is below.
Motivation Great strides are now being made in the world of artificial intelligence toward the automation of tedious tasks, reducing human workloads across a ariety of fields and industries. The workload of our educators is one that desperately needs a reduction, as they are overworked and understaffed. We interviewed several schoolteachers to gather data on their preferences, and conceptualized various solutions to the problem. As such, in this work we plan to develop a user-friendly intelligent tool that reduces the workload of teachers. Our tool’s chosen concept involves the use of natural language processing to grade short form answer assignments.
Methods We offer a fun and interactive game as a way to teach children these important typing skills. Our game will feature the following major components:
  1. Design Process:
      During the design process of our product, we made many changes to improve its accuracy, reliability and ease of use. We created three prototypes, each with improvements and changes made based on testing and feedback.
  2. Handwriting to text functionality:
      The first part of our pipeline was our Handwriting to Text function. We attempted two methods: Object Detection and the pre-trained TrOCR model. The Object Detection method was not robust enough for sentences or multi-word answers, while the TrOCR model resulted in nearly perfect accuracy. The TrOCR model was trained using the IAM handwriting dataset and was able to properly convert entire sentences from handwriting to text.
  3. OpenCV implementation:
      For the third round of prototyping, we implemented a requirement for single-line responses for demonstration urposes. We utilized the OpenCV library to extract handwritten answers from the submitted documents. This method is currently limited in that it only applies to a very specific format with five answers.
  4. GUI design:
      We built our GUI in Streamlit and implemented a multi-page format. The user can advance to the next stage of the process after certain quotas are filled, at which point the "Continue" button becomes available. There is also a "reset" button in case the user decides to start over from the beginning. The app first asks the user to input the number of questions they are grading, after which the app presents a number of text input boxes equivalent to the number of questions given by the user, into which the user enters the correct answers for the assignment.
  5. Evaluation function:
      Our evaluation function uses Sent2Vec, a Python NLP library for sentence embedding. Sent2Vec uses a trained DistilBERT Base language model and the Word2Vec method to encode sentences into vectors of words. Sent2Vec sums the word vectors for each sentence to obtain a vectorization of the complete sentence. From there, the semantic similarity between two sentence vectors can then be calculated using cosine similarity. Our solution compares the "semantic distance" between the correct answer provided by the teacher and each student's answer. This "distance" value was converted into a grade using a manually-tuned gain. The score is then clipped to prevent negative scores from occurring.
Results Our machine learning pipeline for grading short answer questions was successfully developed and tested through multiple prototypes. We were able to achieve high accuracy in converting handwriting to text using the pre-trained TrOCR model, which resulted in a nearly perfect accuracy for our purposes. We also tested the robustness of the model by examining longer phrases and messier handwriting, and found that snipping the answers line by line improved the model's accuracy.

The final version of our app successfully allowed teachers to upload a CSV file containing their answer key, eliminating the need to type in the answers again after every reset. The app also allowed teachers to upload a ZIP file containing the class’ scanned assignments as individual PDFs, which were segmented and converted to text format using OCR.

Our evaluation function successfully compared the “semantic distance” between the correct answer provided by the teacher and each student’s answer, and converted the “distance” value into a grade using a manually tuned gain. The grade was then clipped to prevent negative scores from occurring. We tested the app on a sample dataset of short answer questions from a high school history exam, and found that the app was able to accurately grade each student's answer within minutes.

Overall, our intelligent tool successfully reduced the workload of teachers by automating the grading of short answer questions, saving teachers hours each week in grading. This tool has the potential to revolutionize the field of education by allowing teachers to focus on more important tasks, such as lesson planning and providing individualized support to students.
Final Thoughts The development of our intelligent tool that automates the grading of short answer questions is a significant step towards easing the workload of educators, who are often overworked and understaffed. The results of our product, which enables teachers to upload pictures of their students' short answer questions and receive a grade in minutes, are promising. Our machine learning pipeline can read each student’s picture, convert the handwriting to text, and then grade each answer by comparing it to the answer key that the teacher uploaded. Our product can automatically analyze an entire class’s homework and then present a list of the student’s name and their grade, thus saving teachers hours each week in grading.
